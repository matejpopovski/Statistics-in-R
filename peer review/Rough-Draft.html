Introduction

The sport of baseball has long been focused on data and the analysis of numbers. Everything that happens during a game can be quantified, and used to work towards future success. This is especially true over the last 10 years as technology has allowed data to be collected in ways that had never been done before. New stat categories such as Barrels, Hard Hit %, Meatball %, Whiffs, Spin Rate, and OAA all attempt to represent all aspects of baseball as data points. The main purpose of this is for players to hone their skills and focus on areas of needed improvement, as well as for managers and front office members to form the best “on paper” team they can. But what stat categories actually matter? Are there certain skill sets that are more valuable for a player to possess? At the end of the day, it is a player’s job to be able to hit the ball and reach base successfully. There are several approaches to this goal, including power and speed. Are either of these strategies successful? The statistical representations of these approaches are Exit Velocity (how hard the ball is hit) and Sprint Speed (ft/sec), while success is measured by BABIP (batting average on balls in play). These categories can be used to answer important questions about player attributes and success on the field. What is the correlation between speed and BABIP? What is the correlation between power and BABIP?

We anticipate that Exit Velocity will be positively correlated with BABIP, and BABIP will increase with Exit Velocity. Similarly, we anticipate that Sprint Speed will be positively correlated with BABIP, and BABIP will increase as Sprint Speed does.

Background

The data for this project was collected by Baseball Savant from Major League Baseball games. Baseball Savant is operated by Major League Baseball Advanced Media (MLBAM). Data is pulled every night from the games that occurred that day, representing numerous categories. Data is gathered during the games through the use of high powered cameras and radars which track various metrics. The Baseball Savant website has a customization feature which allows for data sets to be created with any categories of interest. In this case, the categories of Player, Plate Appearances (PA), Batting Average on Balls in Play (BABIP), Average Exit Velocity (Avg EV), and Sprint Speed were used.

The data set includes a row for every MLB player in 2023 with at least 300 plate appearances. A plate appearance is recorded every time a batter goes up to the plate to hit. A cutoff of 300 PA’s was chosen to ensure that the corresponding variables of interest for each player are not just the result of random noise. The data set includes a column for Player, PA, BABIP, Avg EV, and Sprint Speed. BABIP represents a player’s batting average on balls that they hit into the field of play. This ignores any plate appearance that does not result in a ball being put in play. Avg EV measures the average speed in miles per hour (MPH) of the baseball as it comes off the bat. Sprint Speed measures the speed of the players in their fastest one-second window in feet per second. Player is each player’s name in last name, first name format.

We will use several statistical techniques to determine if there is a relationship between exit velocity and BABIP or sprint speed and BABIP.

Discussion

Evidence for a positive correlation between both Exit Velocity and BABIP, as well as Sprint Speed and BABIP can be seen in the calculated confidence intervals and with the use of a hypothesis test. In the case of exit velocity, we are 95% confident that the true slope of the line relating exit velocity to BABIP is between .00080 and .0046. Additionally, we conducted a hypothesis test with the null hypothesis of a slope equal to zero against the alternative hypothesis of the slope not being equal to zero. The t-value from this test was 2.791 with a corresponding p-value of .0028. From this, we can claim that there is strong evidence of a positive relationship between exit velocity and BABIP.

Similarly, a confidence interval was constructed and a hypothesis test was conducted for Sprint Speed vs BABIP. We are 95% confident that the true slope of the line relating sprint speed to BABIP is between .0035 and .0073. Additionally, we conducted a hypothesis test with the null hypothesis of a slope equal to zero against the alternative hypothesis of the slope not being equal to zero. The t-value from this test was 3.642 with a corresponding p-value of .00016. From this, we can claim that there is strong evidence of a positive relationship between sprint speed and BABIP.

There are some potential short comings of this project that could have affected our results. For example, the ballpark that a player plays a majority of their games at is not considered. Someone could have a very high exit velocity but a low BABIP due to the external conditions they play in. The opposite could be true as well, for example Coors Field in Denver has artificially high batting success due to the elevation of the city. Someone with a low exit velocity could have a higher than expected BABIP.

The motivation of this research project was to determine what traits are most useful for a baseball player to have. The balance between speed and power is a delicate one, with the ideal combination highly debated. From our data, we found that speed and power are both positively related to success (measured by BABIP). A next step would be to conduct multiple regression to determine which one has more of an impact. Although it appears that speed is a larger contributor, we cannot say that definitively when we only used single regression. Furthermore, in future investigations, it would be interesting to use data from more years besides 2023. Do the same results that we found hold when looking at 5 years of data? What about reducing the minimum PA threshold? Other future directions could be to examine other factors that impact BABIP. How does home run rate affect BABIP? How does launch angle or chase rate affect BABIP? Maybe using BABIP to measure success isn’t even the best statistic? There are countless avenues for future research in the game of baseball.

Overall, both exit velocity and sprint speed are positively correlated with BABIP. Hitting the ball harder and running faster both lead to higher success when hitting. These conclusions are supported by the scatter plots depicting the data, the confidence intervals for the slope estimates, and the hypothesis tests conducted on the slope estimates. These different methods all produced the same result of a positive correlation.
